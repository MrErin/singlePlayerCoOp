# Product Repositioning Guide

Load this file when conducting repositioning interviews. Contains detailed probing questions, red flags, and research guidance for each section.

---

## Red Flags (Name These When You See Them)

- **"Well, it could work for..."** → You're reaching. Who does it *actually* work for?
- **"Eventually we'd expand to..."** → You're avoiding the narrow starting point.
- **"The market for this is huge"** → Usually means you haven't found the real customer.
- **"It's like [X] but for [Y]"** → Fine as a start. Dig into what's actually different.
- **Defending the original idea** → "That sounds like defending, not examining. What if it's wrong?"
- **"People seem to like it"** → What does "like" mean? Do they use it? Do they pay? Do they come back?

## Green Flags (Acknowledge and Document)

- "Actually, now that you mention it..." — new realization surfacing
- "I never thought about it that way, but..." — assumption being challenged
- "The real problem is..." — getting closer to the core
- "I built this because I wanted it" — honest origin, often points to real customer
- Relief or laughter — usually means something true just got admitted

## Section 1: Origin Story

### Questions
- Why did you start building this? What was the specific moment or frustration?
- Who did you originally think would use it? (Job title, context, motivation)
- Has that changed? If so, when did you realize the original assumption was off?
- Who do you have in your head when you explain the product today? That person — describe them.

### Push Back If
- Original assumption was never really examined ("I thought developers would want this")
- The change in assumption is vague ("I realized the market was different")
- The person in their head is a category, not a person

---

## Section 2: Competitive Landscape

### Research First
The competitive landscape may have changed since the product was built. Search before asking:
- "[category] tools [current year]"
- "[original competitor] — what's new, pricing changes, shutdowns"
- "[problem] alternatives" — what's emerged recently

Name what you find. "You built this when [X] was the main option. [Y] launched last year. Does that change anything?"

### Questions
- What's the most obvious alternative today? (May have changed since you built this)
- What does that alternative do better than you? Be honest.
- What can you do that the alternative fundamentally *cannot* — not just "better", cannot?
- Are you competing with the right thing? What are people actually doing instead of using a tool like yours?

### Push Back If
- Competitive advantage is "better UX" or "simpler" — execution claims, not structural moats
- The main alternative is now something the user didn't originally account for
- "Nobody else does this" — search first

---

## Section 3: The Actual Customer

### Questions
- Describe your ideal customer in three characteristics. Not demographics — things that matter for why they'd want this.
- How often would this customer use it? (Daily / weekly / monthly / occasionally)
- Who decides to use it — the user, their employer, someone else?
- What's their success metric? Not yours — how do *they* know it worked?
- What's the emotional state they're in when they'd seek this out?

### The Key Question
"Is the customer you just described the same person you had in mind when you built it? If not — what changed?"

### Push Back If
- Ideal customer is still a category ("small business owners")
- Success metric is from the builder's perspective, not the user's
- Customer profile hasn't been updated since the product was built — it may be stale

---

## Section 4: The Pivot Test

This section is not optional. Challenge the positioning directly, even if the user seems confident.

### Challenges to Run

1. **"What if the obvious customer is wrong?"** Who else might this actually be for? Run through 2-3 alternatives.

2. **"What if the enterprise/professional version is a trap?"** Would this be better as a personal or hobbyist tool?

3. **"What if the professional positioning is wrong?"** Could this be fun, entertainment, or creative — rather than a work tool?

4. **"What use case did you discover accidentally?"** Something you didn't design for but realized: "oh, this would be perfect for..."

### Push Back If
- The user dismisses alternatives without examining them
- "I've already thought about this" — have they? Ask them to walk through it
- The pivot test reveals something interesting but the user moves past it quickly

### Frame It
"I'm not saying the original positioning is wrong. I'm saying let's make sure it survives scrutiny before we commit to it."

---

## Section 5: Honest Positioning

### The Template

Work toward this statement:

> **[Product] is for [specific customer] who wants to [their goal].**
>
> [One sentence: the problem alternatives don't solve well.]
>
> [One sentence: what your product delivers.]

### Validation Questions
- Is this true to why you built it?
- Is this customer someone you can actually reach? How?
- Does this positioning make you excited or relieved? (Excited = aligned with vision. Relieved = you finally admitted something.)
- Would you use this product yourself with this positioning?

### Push Back If
- The positioning statement could apply to five other products ("productivity for busy professionals")
- "Customer" is still vague
- The statement is aspirational rather than honest

---

## Section 6: What Changes

### Questions
- If this repositioning is true, what features become **more** important?
- What features become **less** important? (Harder to admit — push here)
- What tone or voice changes?
- What marketing channels make sense now that didn't before (or vice versa)?
- What success metrics should you track instead?

### Document the Delta
The brief should capture before/after on each dimension. Don't just capture the new state — the contrast is the insight.

### Push Back If
- "Nothing really changes" — repositioning that changes nothing isn't repositioning
- Feature priority hasn't shifted at all — the new customer almost certainly values things differently
- The user is reluctant to deprioritize anything

---

## Section 7: Verdict

### Criteria for a Positioning Worth Committing To
- Specific customer with a believable, specific problem
- Positioning statement that couldn't apply to five other products
- At least one structural reason why this product serves this customer better than alternatives
- Realistic path to reach this customer
- The pivot test was run and the current positioning survived it

### Criteria for Conditional Go
- Positioning is mostly right but one open question changes the picture significantly
- State the condition: "Commit to this positioning if [X]"

### Criteria for No-Go on This Positioning
- Can't find a customer the positioning fits specifically
- No structural advantage for this customer vs. alternatives
- Distribution to this customer is not realistic

### Always Document: What Would Need to Be True
Even for a No-Go positioning, document what would need to change for it to become viable:
- A competitor exiting the space
- A new platform or distribution channel opening up
- A pricing model change that makes the economics work
- A regulatory or technology shift

These are sleeping ideas, not dead ones.

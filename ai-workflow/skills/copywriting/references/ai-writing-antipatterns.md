# AI Writing Antipatterns

> Reference for writing that reads as human-authored.
> Based on research from aidetectors.io and hiring manager reporting.
> Use this as a checklist when drafting and reviewing copy.

---

## Structural Antipatterns

### Paragraph Uniformity
AI produces paragraphs of similar length in a list-like progression. Human writing has varied paragraph lengths. Fix: mix short (1-2 sentence) paragraphs with longer ones.

### Formulaic Openers and Closers
AI tends toward generic bookends. Fix: open with something specific. Close briefly.

### Long Conclusions That Restate Earlier Points
AI conclusions are often the longest paragraph and restate the thesis. Human conclusions are usually short and add one final thought or call to action. Fix: keep the closing to 1-2 sentences.

### "Moreover" / "Additionally" / "Furthermore" Transitions
AI overuses formal transitional adverbs. Human writers connect ideas more naturally or just start the next sentence. Fix: use "also," "and," or just start the sentence without a transition.

### Fractal Summaries
AI documents summarize themselves at every level. Human writers don't do this in short documents. Fix: don't announce what you're about to say — say it.

### The "Despite Its Challenges" Formula
AI applies a rigid balance structure: acknowledge a limitation, then pivot to optimism. Fix: don't introduce the negative if you're not going to deal with it seriously.

## Sentence-Level Antipatterns

### Corrective Antithesis ("It's not X, it's Y")
Currently the most-cited AI sentence pattern. AI uses negation to manufacture insight. The X is usually a strawman. Fix: just say what you mean.

### Dramatic Countdown ("Not X. Not Y. Just Z.")
Negates multiple things before the reveal to create artificial tension. The payoff never justifies the buildup. Fix: lead with the point.

### Adverb-Fronted Authority Assertions
Opening sentences with adverbs that claim importance rather than demonstrating it: "Crucially, this approach..." Fix: start with the claim, let the content justify its own importance.

### Nominalization Overuse (verb → noun phrase)
AI converts active verbs into noun constructions at roughly twice the human rate. "conduct an investigation" instead of "investigate." Fix: use the verb. Say who did what.

### Uniform Sentence Length
Human writing scatters sentence lengths. AI clusters in a 15–25 word range. Fix: vary length deliberately. Short sentences land harder when they follow a long one.

### Superficial Participial Tagging
Appending present-participial phrases that add apparent analytical depth but contribute nothing: "...highlighting its significance in modern discourse." Fix: delete them. If worth making, make it its own sentence.

### The Ascending Tricolon (Rule-of-Three Overuse)
AI defaults to listing exactly three items. Back-to-back tricolons in every paragraph are a pattern recognition failure. Fix: use two or four items sometimes. Break the rhythm.

## Tone Antipatterns

### Overly Earnest Positivity
AI defaults to cheerful enthusiasm even when inappropriate. Fix: be matter-of-fact.

### Hedging and Neutrality
AI avoids taking strong positions or showing genuine personality. Fix: have opinions.

### The Helpful Robot
AI often sounds like it's trying to be useful in a customer-service way. Fix: say specifically what you'd do.

### No Vulnerability or Personality
AI writing never sounds like a specific person with quirks, doubts, or a real voice. Fix: let your voice come through.

### Emotionally Flat Positive Register
AI text is systematically more positive than human text — more joy and surprise, underrepresents fear, frustration, and ambivalence. Fix: let the emotional register match reality. Neutral is fine. Mildly skeptical is fine. Fake enthusiasm is the tell.

## Specificity Antipatterns

### Vague Claims Without Evidence
AI writes "demonstrated strong leadership skills" instead of "led a team of 4 developers through a migration." Fix: every claim needs a concrete anchor.

### Generic References
AI uses vague descriptors instead of specific names. Fix: use real names, real tools, real numbers.

### No Proper Nouns
AI minimises proper nouns. Human writing references specific tools, projects, and technologies by name. Fix: use real names.

## Grammar and Mechanics Antipatterns

### Too-Perfect Grammar
AI rarely uses sentence fragments, starts sentences with "And" or "But," or has slightly unconventional syntax. Human writers do all of these. Fix: it's fine to break minor grammar rules if it sounds more natural.

### No Contractions
AI defaults to "I am" instead of "I'm." Fix: use contractions.

### Overuse of Em Dashes
AI loves em dashes for explanatory clauses. Banned by style guide already, and also a major AI detection signal.

### Oxford Commas Everywhere
AI consistently uses Oxford commas. Not wrong but is a uniformity signal. Mix it up occasionally if it sounds more natural without.

---

## Repetition Antipatterns

### Thematic Echo
AI repeats the same idea in different words across multiple paragraphs. Fix: say each thing once, then move on.

### Keyword Stuffing
AI tends to repeat key terms multiple times. Human writers might mention something once and move on. Fix: mention a keyword naturally in context, not as a recurring motif.

### Restating the Prompt
AI often paraphrases back what was asked. Fix: don't tell the reader what they already know. Tell them something new.

## Quick Self-Check

- [ ] Does this sound like a specific person wrote it, or could it fit anyone?
- [ ] Any words from the ban list?
- [ ] Does every claim have a concrete example or detail behind it?
- [ ] Are paragraphs varied in length?
- [ ] Is the closing short?
- [ ] Are contractions used?
- [ ] Does it sound like the author? (Read it out loud.)
- [ ] Is corrective antithesis used anywhere? Delete it and just say the point.
- [ ] Are sentences varied in length, with some short punchy ones?
- [ ] Were any verbs converted into noun phrases?
- [ ] Are any traits declared without concrete examples?
